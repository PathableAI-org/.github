# Code of Conduct

## Our Commitment

PathAble is committed to building a respectful, accessible, and inclusive
technical community.

We welcome participation regardless of disability, neurotype, race,
ethnicity, nationality, age, religion, gender identity or expression,
sexual orientation, body size, socioeconomic status, education,
experience level, communication style, or use of assistive technology.

We value lived experience alongside technical expertise. Participation
must not require someone to disclose a disability, diagnosis,
accommodation, or other personal information.

## Expected Behavior

We expect participants to:

- Treat others with dignity, patience, and respect.
- Welcome questions, constructive disagreement, and different experience levels.
- Critique ideas and work without attacking the person responsible for them.
- Respect stated names, pronouns, communication preferences, and boundaries.
- Make reasonable efforts to communicate accessibly and in plain language.
- Accept correction without retaliation or prolonged defensiveness.
- Ask before sharing another person's words, identity, or personal experiences.
- Protect confidential, customer, participant, and security-sensitive information.
- Prioritize safety and community well-being over winning an argument.

## Unacceptable Behavior

Unacceptable behavior includes:

- Harassment, intimidation, threats, stalking, or unwanted attention.
- Discriminatory, derogatory, or ableist language or imagery.
- Personal attacks, insults, ridicule, or deliberately demeaning comments.
- Questioning whether someone is “disabled enough” or demanding proof of disability.
- Unsolicited medical advice or attempts to diagnose another person.
- Disclosing another person's disability, diagnosis, accommodations, identity,
  or personal information without permission.
- Sexualized language, imagery, or attention.
- Deliberately disrupting discussions, reviews, events, or collaborative work.
- Retaliating against someone who raises a concern or participates in an investigation.
- Publishing protected health information, customer information, credentials,
  private communications, or security-sensitive details.
- Using accessibility needs or communication differences to dismiss,
  exclude, or undermine someone.

## Scope

This Code of Conduct applies within PathAble repositories and other
PathAble-managed technical spaces. It also applies when someone is
publicly representing PathAble or its projects.

Repository maintainers may establish additional rules for a particular
project, but those rules may not weaken this Code of Conduct.

## Reporting Concerns

Do not report Code of Conduct concerns through a public GitHub issue.

Report concerns privately to:

**jshilling@pathableai.com**

Include only the information reasonably necessary to understand the
concern. Do not include protected health information, customer data,
credentials, or unrelated personal information.

Reports will be handled as confidentially as reasonably possible.
Information may be shared with the people necessary to assess and
respond to the concern. PathAble will not promise complete
confidentiality when safety, legal obligations, or a fair review require
limited disclosure.

## Enforcement

People responsible for enforcement will review reports in good faith,
avoid conflicts of interest, and consider the context, severity,
history, impact, and safety of those involved.

Responses may include:

- A private clarification or request to change behavior.
- A warning.
- Removal or editing of comments, issues, pull requests, or other content.
- Temporary limits on participation.
- Permanent removal from a project or community space.
- Escalation when safety, legal, employment, or security concerns require it.

Retaliation against anyone who makes a good-faith report or participates
in a review is prohibited.

## Good-Faith Disagreement

Disagreement, critique, and mistakes are not automatically misconduct.
We distinguish good-faith conflict from patterns of harassment,
discrimination, intimidation, or disregard for another person's
boundaries and safety.

Maintainers should prefer proportionate, restorative responses when
appropriate, while retaining the ability to act promptly when someone
may be at risk.
